The Sound of Shininess: Auditory Pitch and the Perception of Gloss

Nguyen & van Buren · Crossmodal perception of gloss. Preregistered · OSF: osf.io/ceypg

Smooth surfaces tend to squeak when rubbed — a physical regularity that the visual system has apparently learned to exploit. We show that concurrent high-pitched sounds automatically make objects look glossier, even when observers are told to ignore the sound and even when visual information alone is sufficient to make the judgment.

Key Findings

  • Experiment 1: Jewelry items rated as significantly glossier when paired with high-pitched bell sounds vs. low-pitched — replicated with pure sine wave tones (d = 0.49, 0.27)
  • Experiment 2: Observers more accurately discriminated glossier objects when the glossier one was paired with a high-pitched sound — even under forced-choice conditions (d = 0.24, 0.27)
  • Effect held across materials (metal, wood, leather) and two different sound types
  • Likely reflects ecological co-occurrence of smooth surfaces and high-pitched sounds, alongside shared arousal/intensity mappings

These findings reveal a new channel through which auditory information shapes visual material judgments — with potential applications in VR, e-commerce, and online exhibit design, where physical inspection is unavailable.
