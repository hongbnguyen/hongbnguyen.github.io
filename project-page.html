Gaze Transplants and Aesthetic Experience

Nguyen & van Buren Attentional dynamics drive aesthetic preferences for scenes — Evidence from 'attentional transplants'.PDF

We investigated how viewing patterns shape aesthetic experience. By analyzing gaze trajectories, we found that attention patterns drive aesthetic disagreement, prediction fluency mediates preference, and same-image specificity matters.

Attentional Transplants: A Data-Driven Attentional Guide Improves UX and Navigation Performance

What if navigation design learned from attention itself? A traveler with a tight connection rushes through an unfamiliar terminal. Signage competes with advertisements. What if the path forward simply... appeared?

By transplanting the gaze patterns of fast, expert navigators onto new travelers, we replace prescriptive signage with empirically learned attentional scaffolds — guiding without directing.

High-Pitched Sounds Make Objects Look Shinier in Online Exhibits

A crossmodal correspondence between auditory pitch and visual gloss perception. When objects are accompanied by higher-pitched sounds, observers rate them as appearing shinier — suggesting that low-level auditory properties can shape visual material perception in digital contexts.

This has implications for the design of online exhibits and e-commerce interfaces, where sound design may serve as an unappreciated channel for shaping how objects appear.

Games as Laboratories for Visual Cognition Research

I helped develop Perception in Virtual Worlds, a curriculum using game environments as a platform for perception research and teaching experimental design. Below are case studies — not completed experiments, but examples of how classic arcade games can isolate perceptual variables in dynamic, embodied contexts.

Tailed Pac-Man

Can a single visual appendage change how vulnerable an agent appears — and how long it survives? I added a simple "tail" to Pac-Man to test whether minimal visual structure alters perceived vulnerability. The tailed agent was caught faster and elicited more abrupt movement patterns.

▶ Play Tailed Pac-Man on itch.io

Color Pong

Does color alter not only how a game feels — but how we play it? Identical Pong environments differing only in background color. Color influenced time spent playing, movement patterns, and performance.

▶ Play Color Pong on itch.io

Maze Width Illusion

If two paths are the same length, can width alone determine which one we choose? In a virtual maze, participants chose between identical-length routes that differed only in width. Wider paths were perceived as shorter and chosen significantly more often.

▶ Play Maze Width Illusion on itch.io

This work speaks to anyone interested in how implicit perceptual mechanisms shape behavior in real-world digital environments — from museum exhibits to airport terminals to the interfaces we use every day.

02 — Social perception

Seeing
minds

How does the visual system extract social meaning — intention, agency, mischief — from surprisingly minimal cues? These projects ask what perception contributes to our understanding of other minds.

Motion, Mischief, and Moral Perception

Nguyen, H. B., et al. The face of mischief.PDF

We explored how minimal visual cues can signal play, mischief, and agency. A distinctive facial expression — asymmetric, weighted to the right side — signals playful rather than hostile intent. This expression may help children learn to distinguish play aggression from real aggression, and its right-side asymmetry appears cross-culturally, even in emoji design.

Key Findings

  • Subtle visual scaffolds signal social intent
  • Perception extracts animacy and goal structure from minimal cues
  • Moral inference is grounded in low-level perceptual processing
  • The mischievous expression is right-asymmetric and cross-culturally consistent

The Efficiency of Perceiving the Intentions Behind Others' Movements

We examined how efficiently observers can detect goal-directed motion from simple animated shapes. The visual system extracts animacy and intentionality rapidly and automatically — with surprisingly fine-grained precision — suggesting that social cognition is grounded in low-level visual processing well before deliberate reasoning begins.

Social cognition doesn't start where perception ends. These projects show that the visual system already performs surprisingly abstract inferences about other minds — long before deliberate reasoning begins.

03 — Physical cognition

Seeing
forces

Vision encodes implicit models of physics — gravity, friction, contact — not by seeing forces directly, but by building predictive models that minimize surprise when objects move. These projects reveal the causal structure hidden inside visual motion.

Published · Cognition 2024
Friction and attention: an implicit model of contact drives spatial orienting
A rotating object touching a surface implies a direction of movement via friction. We show this implied force automatically orients visual attention — even when the object isn't moving. Ceiling contact reverses the effect entirely.
Nguyen & van Buren · Cognition 2024 ↗
Published · JEP:HPP 2023
Visual prioritization of speed changes opposite gravity
Observers are more sensitive to acceleration in upward-moving objects — because upward acceleration violates what gravity predicts. The visual system imputes gravitational force to anticipate motion, then flags deviations from that prediction.
Nguyen & van Buren · JEP:HPP 2023 ↗

These findings suggest that perceptual systems don't just track motion — they maintain internal models of the causal structure of the physical world, allowing them to predict, and be surprised by, the invisible forces that govern how things move.
