Attentional guides for digital environments

Designers have long struggled with guiding attention on websites and in interactive games. The premise of these projects is simple: instead of relying on our intuition about how people should attend, what if we guided users to attend similarly to past users who interacted optimally with the design?

I: Transplanting attention to optimize subjective evaluations of places

Here is the viewing pattern of somebody who gave this scene a relatively low aesthetic rating:

Liked it least

In a recent preprint, I demonstrate that guiding new participants to attend to an image similarly to a person who particularly liked the image causes the new participants to like it too. Meanwhile, guiding participants to attend similarly to a past viewer who disliked the image caused them to like it less — by almost 2 std!

As we discuss in this conference paper ↗, the beauty of this approach is that you don't need any explicit theory about how exactly attention drives user experience. Instead, you leverage "the wisdom of the crowd" to find the way of attending that optimizes for any outcome.

II: Transplanting attention to speed up navigation

I have also used this principle to design a new system to guide users' attention in virtual and mixed reality environments. Click the 'airport navigation' game below, and use WASD and your cursor to try to move to your gate as quickly as possible.

Click to interact · WASD to move · Mouse to look

Pretty hard, right? Maybe you can benefit from seeing the path of the person who navigated fastest to your destination — click the left button to reveal the attentional guide.

Click to interact · WASD to move · Mouse to look · Left-click to reveal guide

There are countless ways in which understanding visual perception and attention can aid interface design — my work demonstrates that this understanding is not always necessary to build a powerful attentional guidance system for any outcome.
