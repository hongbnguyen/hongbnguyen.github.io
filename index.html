About

I study how surprisingly sophisticated implicit knowledge is woven into perception — how the brain encodes invisible forces and social meaning into what looks like simple visual experience.

My work shows that the visual system makes unconscious assumptions about physical forces such as gravity and friction, allowing us to anticipate motion before we explicitly reason about it.

It also demonstrates that we rapidly extract social meaning from minimal cues — seeing animacy, agency, and intention in patterns of movement and facial expression.

A third branch of my research investigates how visual attention and information acquisition shape experience in more complex tasks — from aesthetic judgment to navigation through space.

Overall, my work supports two central claims: that fast, automatic visual processing is more computationally sophisticated than we typically assume, and that seemingly simple acts of looking provide the foundation for higher-level thought.

Invisible Structure

The visual system encodes gravity and friction as priors. Sensitivity to motion depends on these assumptions — we detect speed changes that violate expected forces more readily than those consistent with them.
