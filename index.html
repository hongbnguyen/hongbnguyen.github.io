About

Hi, I'm Hong B. Nguyen, a vision scientist who studies how our visual system quietly weaves physics and psychology into perception. I investigate how simple acts of looking—like watching a wheel spin or a shape glide across a screen—reveal deep, automatic assumptions about forces, goals, and causality that shape perception before we ever start to think.

I'm finishing my Ph.D. in Psychology at The New School for Social Research, where I study how people's visual systems build intuitive models of the physical and social world. My research shows that our visual system is remarkably sophisticated: it predicts how objects will roll, fall, or act—often without our awareness. We're drawn to motion that defies gravity, and even to simple shapes that seem to have intentions.

Beyond the lab, I design experiments that blur the boundaries between perception, aesthetics, and technology. In one project, "attentional transplants," I found that how you look at a scene can make others like it more. I've also explored how facial expressions convey playful intent, why shiny things sound "high-pitched," and how perceptual biases differ across cultures.
