About

Hi! I'm Hong B. Nguyen, a vision scientist who studies how our visual system quietly weaves physics and psychology into the way we see the world. I investigate how seemingly simple acts of looking—like watching a wheel spin or a shape move across a screen—reveal deeply ingrained assumptions about forces, goals, and other causal structures that shape perception long before we start reasoning about them.

I'm currently finishing my Ph.D. in Psychology at The New School for Social Research, where I investigate how people's visual systems build rich causal and social models of the world — often before we've had time to think.

My research shows that our visual system is a lot smarter than we give it credit for. For example, I've discovered that when you watch a spinning wheel, your visual system automatically assumes there's friction and a floor involved, and starts predicting where the wheel will roll next — even if you're not paying attention. Similarly, we're exceptionally good at noticing objects that move against gravity (as any horror movie director already knows), and we have a curious fondness for simple shapes that act like they have goals — like a dart turning toward its target. These perceptual quirks reveal how deeply our knowledge of physics and minds is embedded in the way we see.

Beyond the lab, I love designing experiments that blur the boundaries between vision science, aesthetics, and technology. My work on “attentional transplants,” for instance, shows that how you move your eyes while exploring a landscape can make other people like the same scene more. I've also explored how facial expressions signal playful intent, why shiny things sound “high-pitched,” and how our perceptual biases develop across different cultures and environments.
